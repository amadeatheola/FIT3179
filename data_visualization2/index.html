Rice in Southeast Asia

The backbone of Southeast Asia’s agriculture

Consumption

From 2019 to 2022, global rice consumption steadily increased, reaching nearly 500 million tons. Southeast Asia remained the world’s rice hub, with nations such as Vietnam, Thailand, Myanmar, Cambodia, Indonesia, and the Philippines consuming over 200 kg per person annually. In many of these countries, rice still provides up to three-quarters of daily calories. Pandemic-era stockpiling and the grain’s affordability reinforced its role as both a dietary staple and cultural foundation.

Yield

Despite climate stress and pandemic disruptions, global rice production grew from about 498 to over 515 million tons (milled basis) between 2019 and 2022. Southeast Asia remained a key producer and exporter, with stable yields in Indonesia and the Philippines offsetting drought-affected harvests in Thailand and Vietnam. The region’s resilience ensured a steady global supply, underscoring its vital role in sustaining food security worldwide.

The average southeast asian consumes more than 3 bowls of rice a day, showing how deeply ingrained rice remains in their culture.

Indonesia, Malaysia, Myanmar, and Thailand maintained consistently high consumption levels across 2019–2022, reflecting the grain’s enduring role as the region’s primary staple.

Among these, Myanmar shows a slight upward trend, signaling stronger dietary reliance and possibly improved food availability. In contrast, Laos exhibits a gradual decline, suggesting dietary diversification or reduced rice intake over time.

Meanwhile, Cambodia, Vietnam, and the Philippines sustained moderate to high consumption, all above the global average.

Overall, Southeast Asia’s exceptional dependence on rice is underscore, where most countries consume four to six bowls daily, far exceeding global norms and emphasizing rice’s cultural, nutritional, and economic importance to the region.

From 2015 to 2022, ASEAN countries maintained stable rice production, with Indonesia, Vietnam, and Thailand leading in output and showing gradual improvements in yield through better technology and farm practices. Rice consumption remained consistently high across the region, reinforcing its role as a daily staple and key source of calories. However, nations like Indonesia and the Philippines continued to face production shortfalls, resulting in lower self-sufficiency and higher import reliance.

In contrast, Thailand and Vietnam dominated regional exports, supporting both intra-ASEAN and global rice markets. Despite periodic challenges such as droughts, floods, and economic disruptions, the region overall displayed strong resilience, balancing domestic consumption with export performance and underscoring rice’s central role in Southeast Asia’s food security and agricultural economy.
